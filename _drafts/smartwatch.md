Of late I've come to use two very different kinds of (smart)watch. Currently I prioritize heart rate and step tracking, so I've opted to eschew the typical smartwatch-y things.

# The Contenders

__2018 Apple Watch Sport Series 4 (44 mm)__. Apple's representstive into the smartwatch market sports a beautiful, fully digital face, and must be paired with iPhone. Apple Watch is pretty much an iPhone for your wrist, supporting functionality such as making and receiving calls, text messages, turn-by-turn navigation, and 3rd-party apps. Good thing for us is that the Watch can also function as a heart rate sensor and pedometer.

__2018 Withings Steel HR Sport (40 mm)__. Withings bills the Steel HR Sport as a hybrid smartwatch; i.e., it's _mostly_ an analog face, and won't be able to do most of the things Apple Watch does. However, it's supposed to be health-focused, so heart rate sensing and step tracking? Check.

# Companions

I paired both devices to a different iPhone 8 (2017) running an up-to-date version of iOS 12.

Apple supports 2 companion iOS apps: _Activity_ & _Watch_; I'll be focused on _Health_ as that's where it is possible to review heart rate and step data. I'm vaguely aware of this thing called _HealthKit_, here's what it says [on their website](https://developer.apple.com/healthkit/):

> Integrate HealthKit into your health and fitness apps for iOS and watchOS to create a more seamless user experience. When a customer provides permission for your app to read and write health and activity data to their Health app, your app becomes a valuable data source and can deliver deeply informed health and fitness solutions.

I quoted Apple because Withings ships a companion app for iOS called _Health Mate_...

# Watch face

Apple Watch's digital display use a high-resolution, full-colour organic light emitting diode (OLED) in a rounded rectangle. I find myself exaggerating my wrist flicks lest the display doesn't turn on. I also found more scratches from seemingly run-of-the-mill knocks.

Meanwhile, the Steel HR Sport is an analog face with two sub-dials (one digital).

FIXME: Stroke width is a bit thin for my subjective legibility however, especially in strong daylight. Furthermore, neither the clock hands nor the hour markings are luminous, so at nighttime an extra button press is required.

# Battery life

The Withings Steel HR Sport has a claimed battery life of 25 days, amazing relative to the chore of having to charge Apple Watch daily.

# Integration

I much prefer the truly seamless Steps data syncs between Apple Watch/Health.

Meanwhile, the Steel HR/Health Mate companion app takes a noticeably longer turnaround time to eventually become visible in Health; e.g., T+45 minutes when I checked. There appears to be no way to force an update, further exacerbating the timeliness of said info. Also, I noticed that Heart Rate data  _never_ syncs even though the relevant authorizations are granted (Settings > Privacy > Health > Health Mate > Allow "Health Mate" to write Data: > Heart Rate). Health Mate graphs it out nicely enough, but I'd much prefer it in Health.

# Data residency

FIXME: Apple Watch's health data eventually makes its way into my iPhone; i.e., it is local. Meanwhile, Withings has a published [Health Mate API](http://oauth.withings.com/oauth2/); i.e., my personal health data is stored somewhere in the cloud.

# Summary

Overall, I think both devices can stand on their own merits. Now if only someone could make something that ticks all the boxes:

1. A stylish-looking watch bearing an scratch-proof round face;
1. Weeks-long battery life;
1. Easily interchangeable straps;
1. Seamless fitness tracking (Steps, Heart Rate) integration; i.e., near real-time updates both onscreen and in-app.
